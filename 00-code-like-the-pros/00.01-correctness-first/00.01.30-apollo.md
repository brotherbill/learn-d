# 00.01.30 — The Apollo Guidance Computer  
The Greatest Correctness‑First System Ever Built

The Apollo Guidance Computer (AGC) is the strongest historical proof that correctness‑first engineering works.  
It is the gold standard for reliability, discipline, and mission‑critical software design.

No other system demonstrates correctness‑first principles as clearly, as successfully, or as dramatically.

---

## 1. The Mission: Land Humans on the Moon  
### Failure Was Not an Option

The AGC was responsible for:

- navigation  
- guidance  
- trajectory correction  
- lunar descent  
- rendezvous  
- re‑entry  

If the software failed, astronauts died.

There were no retries.  
There were no patches.  
There was no “debug in production.”

Correctness‑first was mandatory.

---

## 2. The Team: Small, Disciplined, and Focused

The AGC software team was tiny:

- fewer than 40 developers  
- working in a single building  
- with no modern tools  
- no IDEs  
- no debuggers  
- no version control  
- no automated testing frameworks  

Yet they produced one of the most reliable systems ever built.

Their secret was correctness‑first discipline:

- contracts  
- invariants  
- stable foundations  
- incremental construction  
- mechanical reasoning  
- mathematical clarity  

They did not rely on speed.  
They relied on correctness.

---

## 3. The Method: Contracts and Invariants Everywhere

Every AGC routine had:

- **preconditions** — what must be true before execution  
- **postconditions** — what must be true after execution  
- **invariants** — what must remain true at key points  
- **contracts** — the agreement between caller and callee  

These were not optional.  
They were the backbone of the system.

The AGC team understood:

> **If a system is not correct, its performance is irrelevant.**  
> — Dr. Bertrand Meyer

The AGC was not fast.  
It was correct — and correctness made it unstoppable.

---

## 4. The Hardware: Slow, Limited, and Perfectly Understood

The AGC had:

- **2,048 words of RAM**  
- **36,864 words of ROM**  
- **1 MHz clock speed**  
- **no floating‑point hardware**  
- **no luxury of waste**

And most importantly:

### **AGC Word Size: 15 Bits + Parity**

The AGC used a **15‑bit word** for all arithmetic and logic.  
A **16th bit** was stored physically, but used only for **parity checking**, not computation.

This choice shaped the entire system:

- arithmetic was integer‑only  
- overflow behavior was well‑defined  
- invariants were simple and enforceable  
- memory layout was predictable  
- correctness was easier to reason about  
- every bit was accounted for  

The AGC’s word size forced discipline — and discipline produced correctness.

---

## 5. Why 15 Bits Was Chosen  
### A Word Size That Enforces Correctness

The AGC designers chose 15 bits because:

- it was large enough for navigation math  
- it was small enough to fit in limited memory  
- it simplified overflow detection  
- it reduced hardware complexity  
- it made invariants easy to define  
- it made correctness easier to guarantee  

A larger word size would have required more hardware, more power, and more complexity.  
A smaller word size would have made navigation impossible.

15 bits was the correctness‑optimal choice.

The AGC team followed a rule that Learn‑D teaches today:

> **A program that compiles, runs, and produces correct output

This prevented:

- regressions

- ghost bugs  
- emergency patches  

The AGC was always in a correct state.  
New code never destabilized the system.

## 7. The Result: Zero Software‑Caused Mission Failures

Across all Apollo missions:

- lunar orbit  
- lunar landing  
- lunar ascent

Even during Apollo 11’s famous 1201/1202 alarms, the AGC behaved exactly as designed:

- it shed non‑critical tasks  
- it preserved critical guidance  
- it maintained invariants  
- it kept the spacecraft safe  

Correctness‑first saved the mission.
